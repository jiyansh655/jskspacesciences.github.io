Yellow dwarf



Yellow dwarf is a G type star has a mass of ≈ about 0.84 - 1.85 solar masses and the temperature between 5,300 - 6000 kelvin. Our nearest star sun is also a yellow dwarf



Red giants



A red giant is a star that has exhausted the supply of hydrogen in its core and has begun thermonuclear fusion of hydrogen in a shell surrounding the core. They have radii tens to hundreds of times larger than that of the Sun. However, their outer envelope is lower in temperature, giving them a reddish-orange hue. Mass of a red giant ≈ 0.3-8 solar masses and the surface temperature around 5,000 K (4,700 °C; 8,500 °F) or lower.



Blue supergiants



A blue supergiant (BSG) is a hot, luminous star. Blue supergiants are found towards the top left of the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram, above and to the right of the main sequence. Mass of a blue supergiant ≈ 10 and 100 solar masses and temperature ≈ 10,000–50,000 K



Red supergiants



Red supergiants (RSGs) are stars with a supergiant luminosity class of spectral type K or M. They are the largest stars in the universe in terms of volume, although they are not the most massive or luminous. Betelgeuse and Antares are the brightest and best known red supergiants (RSGs), indeed the only first magnitude red supergiant stars. Mass of red supergiants ≈ more than 100 solar masses and temperature ≈ below 4,100 K because they have the least temperature

1. Interstellar cloud



Stars are born within enormous, cold, dense clouds of gas and dust. the process of star formation may be triggered if something disturbs the cloud.



2. Fragments forms

3. A protostar forms

Now a protostar forms. gravity pull material inti its core, where density, pressure and temperature builds up.



3. Spinning disc

The increasing mass at center creates a gravitational pull, drawing ever more gas and dust inwards.



4. A star is born
